Devola and Popola are twins from the videogame NieR, and its sequel, NieR:Automata.

In NieR, they are the matriarchs of the protagonist's home village. Popola resides in a library of books from a past civilization that only she can read, and handles most of the more complicated tasks. Devola can be found first outside near the fountain, then later in the tavern, and is quite carefree in comparison. She spends her days playing a lute, a tune which serves as the background track for the village, and, if you're close enough nearby, you can hear her sing along.

It's a song she doesn't really understand.

Their main functions as videogame NPCs are to provide quests Popola, with her position and access to vast amounts of knowledge, is a stopping point every now and again along the storyline. Devola with her free time listens and chats with villagers who pass by, and relays to us optional sidequests we can take. These sidequests make up a fair portion of the game's playtime, perhaps half the total hours by the end. These sidequests are the flavor and worldbuilding for the game's story - just like how Devola is the flavor to Popola.

One sidequest chain involves collecting strange ingredients to brew a special alcoholic drink allows you to ask Popola to come out to the inn and sing a duet version with Devola. Devola apparently doesn't get any though, because under the influence she "gets a bit too friendly".

In Automata they are travellers who happened to be in the area. They are no longer matriarchs, but outcasts, for a crime everyone everywhere will forever find unforgivable. But, they have unique skills, so they're begrudgingly allowed to be around to do work no one else can do. They're the same old Devola and Popola though - what could have happened?


The song Devola (and Popola) sings, which is never explicitly named in-universe, has the title Song of the Ancients. Over the course of the two games the song makes appearances in multiple versions at multiple times. In this player's opinion, this song, its various remixes, and its implementations make it the best piece of music ever composed.
